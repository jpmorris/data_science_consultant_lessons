## Theoretical Source of Performance Gains

### 1. Pre-training

#### Kaplan 2020

Kaplan 2020, "Scaling Laws for Natural Language Models":
[https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.08361](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.08361)

- There are three knobs to turn to improve model performance:
  - Data
  - Parameters
  - Compute (GPU-hours)
- Your **compute budget** is the product of these three, and you can trade off
  between them to achieve better performance.
- Kaplan empirically found the power coefficients for these three factors.

> The test loss of a Transformer trained to autoregressively model language can
> be predicted using a power-law when performance is limited by only either the
> number of non-embedding parameters $N$, the data set size $D$, or the
> optimally allocated compute budget $C_{min}$:
>
> 1. For models with a limited number of parameters, trained to convergence on
>    sufficiently large data sets:
>    $$L(N) = (N_c/N)^{\alpha_N}; \quad \alpha_N \sim 0.076, \quad N_c \sim 8.8 \times 10^{13} \text{ (non-embedding parameters)}$$
> 2. For large models trained with a limited data set with early stopping:
>    $$L(D) = (D_c/D)^{\alpha_D}; \quad \alpha_D \sim 0.095, \quad D_c \sim 5.4 \times 10^{13} \text{ (tokens)}$$
>    3.Wen training with a limited amount of compute, a sufficiently large
>    dataset, an optimally-sized model, and a sufficiently small batch size:
>    $$L(C_{min}) = (C^{min}_c / C_{min})^{\alpha^{min}_C}; \quad \alpha^{min}_C \sim 0.050, \quad C^{min}_c \sim 3.1 \times 10^{8} \text{ (PF-days)}$$
>
> — Kaplan et al., _Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models_ (2020)

![Kaplan Compute Budget](images/kaplan-compute-budget.png)

- An interesting finding is that these laws are robust to architectural design
  and depend weakly on depth and width.

#### Chinchilla 2022

[https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.15556](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.15556)

Chinchilla found that Kaplan was wrong about being parameter skewed

- Kaplan said the optimal allocation was heavily parameter-skewed:
  $$N_{opt} \sim C^{0.73}, \quad D_{opt} \sim C^{0.27}$$
- Chinchilla found the correct relationship is **equal scaling**:
  $$N_{opt} \sim C^{0.5}, \quad D_{opt} \sim C^{0.5}$$
- The practical rule of thumb: $D_{opt} \approx 20 \times N$ — **20 tokens per
  parameter**
- Proof of concept: Chinchilla (70B params, 1.4T tokens) outperformed Gopher
  (280B), GPT-3 (175B), and Megatron-Turing NLG (530B) — all much larger models
  — using the same compute budget
- Implication: most pre-2022 models were **massively undertrained**, not
  undersized

#### Entropy Floor - Some Information theory

Any natural language will have some **entropy floor**, which is the inherent
unpredictability in the language.

Compare:

> I went to the xxxx.

but this unpredictability is an average, consider:

> To be or not to be, that is the xxxx.

```
  "I went to the ___"
  ├── store (p=0.3)  ← real option
  ├── market (p=0.25) ← real option
  ├── hospital (p=0.2) ← real option
  ├── xkzqp (p=0.0) ← not a real option
  └── ...
```

$$H(X_t \mid \text{context}) = -\sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x \mid \text{context}) \log_2, p(x \mid \text{context})$$

Where for a language model, "context" = all prior tokens
$(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_{t-1})$, so more fully:

$$H(X_t \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1}) = -\sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1}) \log_2, p(x \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1})$$

Claude Shannon estimated the entropy (floor) for English to be around 1.3 bits
per character, which means each token has about 3 coin flips of genuine
unpredictability. This mean the model is choosing between 8 options on average.

- Code has a lower entropy floor, especially at the function- and line-level for
  some cases. At higher levels of complexity the entropy floor is higher.

Entropy and performance of completions can be assessed at levels from 'atomic'
to multi-step complex. The most atomic completion is next-word completion, the
complex ones may be ones that ask to add a complex function to a 100,000 line
codebase. Kaplan is relevant to all levels because models can still improve at
all levels, but as the complexity increases so does the entropy-floor. For small
functions the entropy may be zero, at higher level there can be a lot of
inherent randomness that can't be avoided.

---

| Level              | Example                             | Entropy Floor | Notes                                                     |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------------- | ------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| Token/syntax       | `for i in range(`                   | Near zero     | Grammatically constrained                                 |
| Atomic function    | Sort a list, fibonacci              | Very low      | One correct answer or small equivalence class             |
| Module/service     | REST API for user auth              | Low-medium    | Many valid implementations, verifiable by tests           |
| Full application   | "Build me a CRM"                    | High          | Specification is in natural language — entropy lives here |
| Novel architecture | "Design a distributed system for X" | Very high     | Open-ended, no ground truth                               |

### 2. Supervised Fine-Tuning/ Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) Scaling

["Scaling Laws for Reward Model Overoptimization - Gao et al. 2024"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.10760)

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), shows the model examples of good results, RHLF
scaling involves training a reward model to optimize the model's behavior based
on human feedback. This is an active area of research. Gao et al. found a
<mark class="red">logarithmic scaling law for performance with a high risk of
overoptimization in the vein of Goodhart's Law</mark> ("when a measure becomes a
target, it ceases to be a good measure"). Again, this is an empirical
observation and may not be a fundamental law. Reinforcement Learning by
Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) is another method that also tuns weights.

However, both SFT and RHLF could be a method that is used by the labs to
increase performance.

### 4. Inference-time/Chain of Thought Scaling

Instead of training bigger models, what if you give a model more time to think
and have it self-reflect (Chain of Thought prompting)?

["Scalling LLM Test-Time Compute Optimally" - Snell et al. 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.03314)

Empirically, currently, the performance of inference-time scaling
<mark class="red">scales logarithmically with the amount of compute used at
inference time, meaning ever-greater cost for diminishing performance
gains</mark>. However, this is less a fundamental law than an empirical
observation. <mark class="green">Learned search, process reward models, and
iterative self-improvement advancements all may break this logarithmic
performance trend.</mark>

## Source and Forecasts of Current Performance Gains

### Entropy Floor Limits

Again, entropy can be assessed at various levels: character, token, word,
sentence, paragraph, function, module, program, system. Manifestly, the models
are doing well for the most atomic cases, such as functions. In some, of these
cases the entropy may be zero and <mark class="red">the models are hitting the
entropy floor</mark>. It is possible that <mark class="red">the models have also
largely hit the entropy floor at the higher levels as well</mark>. Here the
entropy floor is higher because there are many more options, but that doesn't
mean the models haven't already reached the floor.

Pre-training does have a strong power law, but <mark class="red">the lack of
additional data, and the lack of large pre-training runs by the big labs suggest
that performance for pre-training has either been largely saturated or is at
least no longer low-hanging fruit</mark>.

### Post-entropy: Search Problem

Once reaching the entropy floor at all scales even assuming prefect performance
of the model, there still a finite number of valid final states that need to be
discovered via classic search. The search is not **optimization search**, find
the best solution, but **satisficing search**, find any solution that meets
requriements.

![Traveling Salesman Search](images/traveling-salesman-search.png)

- The solution space scales with complexity, and sometimes dramatically so
- The solution space is reduced by specifying requirements
- Even with complete requirements specified there is still inherent decisions in
  the solution space
- <mark class="green">classic search can be improved by new methods.</mark>
- Unlike most classical search problems ("Check if AlphaGo won the Go game") it
  is expensive to validate (entire suite of unit tests).

> "Programming is requirements specification"
>
> -- Unknown/Many

However, imperfect context means the agent must search with incomplete
information. Without all specifications defined at the outset the agent searches
spaces with many branches and will usually find the sub-optimal solution.

![Feb 2026 Google Trends "Unit Tests"](images/feb-2026-unit-tests.png)

In practice, the user specifies a condition for validation (Did the program do
what I want it to do). and the agent attempts to meet that condition. More
rigorously, a user would specify a great number of unit tests. However, the
difficulty in specifying unit tests is that complete edge and corner cases are
unknown, so the agent must explore solution space with incomplete information.
The quality of the search is bounded by the quality of the Oracle (the tests).

Also <mark class="red">some requirements can't be specified rigorously, in
particular non-functional requirements</mark>, which will not constrain the
solution space further. <mark class="red">One of the hardest to specify is good
code design and design patterns, and these aspects the models struggle with the
most.</mark>

### The Source of Improvement: Inference/Chain of Thought (CoT), RLHF, and Engineering

> "2024–2025 agency training could also be a one-time boost from picking
> low-hanging fruit, in which case horizon growth will slow once these gains are
> exhausted."
>
> -- METR (Kwa et al.)

So why do benchmarks keep getting better? Why is the METR plot increasing? Why
are some models better than others? Why can't the models solve 3-day human tasks
yet? This is probably not because of pre-training, it's possible that the models
are close to the entropy floor at most scales, but either way because of lack of
data, because models are at entropy floor, or because it's not low-hanging fruit
the labs are not running many pre-training runs. Therefore the models are
improving and the models differentiate based on Inference-time/CoT improvements
and RHLF improvements.

The two ways to improve models via scaling is:

- Scale the **Inference/Chain of Thought** - the labs simply make the models
  think longer and give them more compute. However <mark class="red">because
  this scaling is logarithmic (without a breakthrough) there are rapid
  diminishing returns</mark>.
- RLHF - occurs after pre-training and fine-tuning which encodes human
  preference and this may be a point at which they are encoding human learning
  heuristics or general knowledge of problem solving in domains like coding.
  <mark class="red">There appears to be very limited scaling here, and a risk of
  **Goodhart's Law** overfitting.</mark>

The other non-scalable methods rely on engineering:

- More tools
- Better memory management
  - <mark class="red">There are diminishing returns with this method and a high
    risk of overfitting after some point</mark>
- Prompt-time engineering which is catered to various use cases and for getting
  better answers

### Forecasting Improvements

So given the above, baring some major breakthrough, what can we expect from
these models in the future? So what are some short-to-medium-term predictions
can we make for these models. We can largely rule out pretraining as the
incremental improvements. The labs are most likely tweaking engineering and RHLF
to make continual gains. The fact that they don't have massive GPT-3-to-4 level
gains shows that they are doing lots of tweaks with tons of knobs with many
settings (engineering, RHLF, etc) and one continuous knob (how long for
cot/inference) The big question is when these methods will saturate performance
resulting in a performance plateau.

### Other Current Limitations

#### Context Window

The fundamental barrier is the quadratic attention problem:

$$\text{Attention cost} \propto O(n^2)$$

A single attention pass scale quadratically. We are currently at 1-2 Million
tokens context with Grok, Google, and Anthropic. <mark class="red">Expanding
beyond this has many issues including economic cost, exploding memory
requirements, and degraded performance.</mark>

#### Continual Learning

Models suffer from <mark class="red">an inability to learn continuously</mark>.
Improvements of the models do happen at training time and with RHLF. The RHLF
method involves slightly nudging the weights slightly. RHLF is a very slow,
imperfect version of continual learning. <mark class="green">Continual learning
would be a major breakthrough</mark>, but <mark class="red">there doesn't seem
to be any discovery yet of how to learn continuously and it would probably take
a completely different architecture or method.</mark>

This last one is important. It is commonly said, "Just wait these models will
get better". Without denying the possibility, it is intellectually lazy not to
probe the limit. We could have said the same thing one or 2 years ago. We should
think hard about why these models can't find new science from all that it knows.
It's lazy to just assume it will happen.

### Performance Gains Summary

In order for models to improve there needs to be some process you can scale. In
order to scale performance generally a continuous modification (such as compute)
is needed for continual improvement. Alternatively, solving a solution space of
new discoveries may allow to use existing scaling laws to reach ever-greater
performance gains. In terms of the former, several scaling methods seem to have
current limitations for long-term growth (baring important discoveries).

| Scaling method                     | Ceiling type                    | Status                            |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| Pre-training (data/params/compute) | Entropy floor + data exhaustion | Near ceiling                      |
| RLHF                               | Goodhart's Law / proxy limit    | Diminishing returns               |
| Inference/CoT                      | Logarithmic                     | Active, rapid diminishing returns |
| Context window                     | Quadratic architectural barrier | Stuck ~1M tokens                  |
| Architectural discoveries          | Unknown                         | Low prior probability             |

These limits in scaling may yet be overcome. But if they are not, we can expect
the performance of the models to eventually plateau. These potential limits and
architectural limitations result in several current deficiencies in the models.
Whether the current performance or additional performance gains before plateau
result in major job losses is a question we explore next.

#### The Signal Ceiling Insight

A raw pretrained base model — before any post-training — would not place in the
top 100 of any major benchmark leaderboard. It hallucinates freely, has no
instruction-following ability, is sycophantic by default, and gives inconsistent
answers. Everything between that and a deployed model is an engineering stack:
RLHF, Constitutional AI, chain-of-thought elicitation, RAG, tool calling, system
prompts, factuality tuning, output filters.

This leads to a critical distinction:

> **Pretraining establishes the signal ceiling — the maximum knowledge and
> reasoning capacity that can possibly be extracted. Post-training engineering
> is noise reduction — extracting a cleaner signal from a noisy base.**

Noise reduction cannot raise the signal ceiling. When people say "models are
getting better," they are often describing improvements in noise reduction, not
a higher ceiling. The benchmarks measure extracted signal, not the ceiling
itself — which is why ARC-AGI-3 at 0.37% AI vs 100% human is a more honest
signal than leaderboard scores on benchmarks that are sensitive to post-training
tricks.

The base model itself is trained on the **weighted average of human knowledge**
as it appears on the internet: Reddit (40% of training citations), Wikipedia,
books, code, forums. It learns what is stated frequently and confidently, not
what is true. A false claim repeated confidently across 1,000 Reddit threads is
learned more strongly than a true claim appearing only in a single peer-reviewed
paper. The training signal is plausibility and frequency, not ground truth —
exactly as unit tests are a proxy for code correctness but not code quality.

The implication: the model's factual ceiling is the average of human knowledge
production, not the best of it. SimpleQA Verified finds the best current models
wrong on 28-45% of simple factual questions — not because the engineering
failed, but because the signal ceiling was set by the training distribution.

organization can provide.

```
1. There will be AI (before long, absent defeaters)
2. If there is AI there will be AI+ (soon after, absent defeaters)
3. If there is AI+, there will be AI++ (soon after, absent defeaters)
________
3. There will be AI++ (before too long, absent defeaters)
```

If we can build an AI that can make a better AI that can make a better AI and so
on, then we are on a glide path to AGI and ASI.

Classically we've had AutoML since around 2015 and Neural Architecture Search
(NAS) since 2017. So we've already had search algorithms to look for better
models, the later being more applicable to foundational models.

The current evidence that this process is at least being attempted by RL-Bench
as part of METR which asks agents to improve the loss of AI models or make known
changes and claims from labs that they are using AI to improve AI.

But the current AI-improving AIs are the classical search type: the AI changes
the number of parameters, it iterates on known perturbations of existing models.
This is a classical search problem (maybe more efficient because model
reasoning). But this is not the sort of ground-breaking exploration that it took
to discover the Transformer (and even the Transformer is debatable on how
paradigm shifting it was)

NAS has been attempted for years, and 50,000-150,000 AI researchers have been
working for 8 years since the transformer paper. This is tens to hundreds of
hours of work trying to find a solution, and the type of thinking is the
paradigm shifting-sort not classical search. The Transformer was discovered by 8
researchers, Special Relativity was discovered by one man. This all suggests
that the bar is far higher for AI+.

So what stops RSI, if anything does? Chalmers calls them possible 'defeaters':

- Algorithmic defeaters — some problems have no better algorithm regardless of
  intelligence
- Complexity defeaters — some search spaces are exponentially hard; more
  intelligence doesn't help, this is true for both classical search and paradigm
  shifting searches (like trying to solve high-energy physics)
- Paradigm defeaters — the next step requires a conceptual frame that the
  current system cannot generate from within its existing frame

# Summary

## Three-Horizon Outlook

### Short Term: Tech Job Risk (Now — ~2028)

AI is real and improving, primarily on well-specified, verifiable coding and
information tasks. The disruption is already visible in specific pockets:

- **Junior SWE hiring is compressing — but causation is contested.**
  Brynjolfsson et al. report -16% employment for ages 22-25 in AI-exposed
  occupations, and the Anthropic labor market study finds a 14% reduced hiring
  rate for the same cohort post-ChatGPT. However, this trend predates GPT-4 and
  is continuous with post-pandemic correction: tech firms over-hired in
  2020-2022 and cut aggressively from late 2022 onward — coincident with but not
  clearly caused by AI. No clean structural break appears at the GPT-4 release
  (March 2023), and there is no firm-level evidence that early AI adopters cut
  junior hiring faster than non-adopters. Junior workers are plausibly the most
  vulnerable, but current data cannot cleanly separate AI displacement from
  secular post-pandemic correction.
- **Customer service is the clearest displacement signal.** H-γ=0.86,
  Brynjolfsson RCT: +35% productivity for junior workers, NBER finds actual
  employment decline.
- **Data scientists face execution-layer compression.** EDA, feature
  engineering, model training, visualization, and literature review are rapidly
  AI-augmented. A senior DS with AI tools can do in hours what took weeks.
  Junior roles where execution dominates the job description are most at risk.
- **What is NOT happening yet:** broad white-collar displacement, meaningful job
  losses outside tech and customer service, or any evidence of AI pushing the
  frontier of knowledge.

- **The slop problem is real and underappreciated.** AI generates plausible,
  locally coherent code but struggles to maintain global consistency across a
  large codebase. Vibecoding a greenfield app works; vibecoding Gmail produces a
  fragile, untestable, unmaintainable product. The model cannot hold the full
  architectural context of a large system, cannot reason about non-functional
  requirements (performance, security, design patterns), and has no persistent
  memory of the decisions that shaped the codebase. Each session starts fresh.
  The result is code that works in the demo and degrades under real use. This is
  partially a context window problem and partially a "good code" specification
  problem — models are not trained on whether code is well-designed, only on
  whether it runs. Until persistent codebase memory and design-quality signals
  are solved, AI-generated production code at scale requires significant human
  architectural oversight.

**The honest short-term picture:** AI is a powerful junior colleague for
verifiable, well-specified tasks. It is not a drop-in replacement for roles
anchored in organizational judgment, relationships, and accountability.

### Near-Term Predictions (~12—24 Months)

#### Benchmarks

| Benchmark                        | Current                    | Prediction                    | Notes                                                                                     |
| -------------------------------- | -------------------------- | ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Synthetic (MMLU, HumanEval etc.) | Near ceiling               | Continue improving            | Goodhart's Law in full effect — improving on the test                                     |
| METR task horizon                | ~7-month doubling          | Likely continues but may slow | Watch for plateau; one-time RLHF/agent gains may be exhausting                            |
| ARC-AGI-1                        | ~88% (o3)                  | Essentially solved            | No longer a meaningful signal                                                             |

ARC-AGI-3 is the one to watch. It tests interactive reasoning in novel
environments — explore, adapt, update — rather than static pattern matching. At
0.37% vs 100% human, it's the most honest current signal of how far we are from
general reasoning. This does not improve fast.

#### The AI-Aware World: MCP and Agentic Interfaces

This is probably the most certain near-term prediction: **everything will expose
an AI interface.** MCP (Model Context Protocol) crossed 97 million installs in
March 2026 and was donated to the Linux Foundation as the foundational standard.
It is becoming the USB-C of AI — any tool, any agent, any system.

- AWS CLI, Terraform, and well-documented systems already work well with agents
  because the documentation is rich and the interface is clean. Edge languages
  (Neo4j Cypher, obscure DSLs) lag because training data is thin.
- In the next 12 months: every major software vendor will ship an MCP server or
  AI-native interface. Your browser, your IDE, your ERP, your cloud console. The
  question shifts from "does this tool have an API?" to "does this tool have an
  agent interface?"
- **Agent-to-agent communication** is the next layer: an MCP server that is
  itself an agent, negotiating with other agents. A travel agent MCP negotiating
  with a booking agent MCP. This is emerging now and will be widespread within
  18 months.
- **The AI-aware codebase** will emerge as a design pattern: structured
  comments, semantic decision logs, architecture documents written for agent
  consumption. Teams that build AI-readable codebases will have a significant
  productivity advantage.

#### The Slop Problem: Solvable?

Partially, and conditionally. The slop problem has two components:

1. **Persistent memory / codebase state** — partially solvable. MCP-connected
   memory stores, persistent vector indices of codebases, and session continuity
   are active product development areas. Within 12 months, agents will have
   materially better codebase awareness than today. This does not fully solve
   the problem but raises the ceiling.

2. **"Good code" as a training signal** — harder. Models are not trained on
   whether code is well-designed, maintainable, or follows design patterns —
   only on whether it runs and passes tests. There is no easy ground truth for
   code quality the way there is for correctness. Without a verifiable oracle
   for design quality, this is not fixed by more training data.

**The conditional:** If persistent memory improves AND verification loops
tighten (better test coverage, linting, static analysis in the agent loop), slop
is reduced enough that the remaining bottleneck is requirements translation.
That gets you to: "AI writes decent code, human specifies what to build and
reviews architecture." This is not far from where we are — the bar to entry
lowers further but the expert-in-the-loop requirement remains.

#### What the Labs Are Actually Doing — A Telling Signal

Anthropic just released Claude Mythos 5 — a 10T parameter model focused
specifically on **cybersecurity and coding**. OpenAI has Sora, agentic CLIs,
o3-mini. Google has Gemma open weights and domain-specific releases.

**The user's observation is correct and underappreciated:** if ASI were 18
months away, none of this makes sense. You don't spend engineering resources on
a video generator or a cybersecurity product when you're 18 months from
rewriting the laws of intelligence. Productization is revealed preference — the
labs' own behavior says they expect a medium-term competitive landscape, not
imminent singularity. You don't build a moat when the flood is coming tomorrow.

#### AlphaEvolve: A Different Paradigm Worth Watching

AlphaEvolve (DeepMind) is architecturally distinct from everything else and
deserves attention. It combines:

- **LLM for hypothesis generation** (propose algorithm variants)
- **Evolutionary search** (select, mutate, propagate best solutions)
- **Verifiable oracle** (check if the algorithm is provably correct and better)

Results so far: first improvement to matrix multiplication in 56 years (Strassen
1969), kissing number problem in 11D advanced, 20% of open combinatorics
problems surpassed known solutions, 0.7% of Google's worldwide compute recovered
via Borg scheduler optimization.

**Why this matters:** the verifiable oracle is the key. AlphaFold worked because
protein structure is verifiable experimentally. AlphaEvolve works because
algorithm correctness is formally checkable. Coding agents work better than
science agents for the same reason — unit tests are an oracle.

The pattern: **LLM + search + verifiable oracle = real discovery.** Without the
oracle you get confabulation. With it, you can make genuine progress within
formally checkable domains. The frontier question is which scientific domains
have cheap enough oracles to apply this pattern. Drug binding has one (wet lab).
Materials science has one (DFT simulation). Open-ended theory does not.

#### What Comes After Agents? Speculative

The chatbot → agent transition was fast because it was a natural extension: give
a chatbot tools and a loop. What's the next natural extension?

- **Multi-agent** — already here, more of the same, scaling issues documented
- **Specialized models** — likely in 12—18 months. If general capability
  plateaus, labs differentiate by domain. Coding agent, science agent, legal
  agent — each fine-tuned on domain-specific verifiable tasks. Anthropic's
  cybersecurity focus is the first signal of this.
- **Memory as a first-class primitive** — agents with persistent, structured,
  searchable memory across sessions. The missing piece for long-horizon work.
  This is engineering, not research — it will happen.
- **AlphaEvolve-style systems** applied to new domains with oracles — compiler
  optimization, drug design, materials discovery. Not AGI, but genuine
  scientific contribution in formally verifiable domains.
- **Embodied AI / robotics** — learning from physical interaction rather than
  text. Different training signal entirely. Early results from Figure, 1X, and
  Boston Dynamics are promising but 3—5 years from broad deployment.
- **World models (LeCun's JEPA direction)** — genuinely different paradigm. If
  transformers hit their ceiling, this is the candidate replacement. No lab has
  made this work at scale yet. Low prior for 12-month horizon.

**The honest prediction:** the next 12—24 months are engineering, not science.
Better memory, better interfaces, better verification loops, more specialized
models, MCP everywhere. The current technology still hasn't propagated through
the economy — that diffusion will drive most of the observable change.
Augmentation over automation. The scientific breakthrough (if it comes) is not
on a 12-month schedule.

#### The Signal Ceiling and the Engineering Stack

One of the most underappreciated facts about current AI: **a raw pretrained base
model would not rank in the top 100 of any major benchmark.** Every impressive
capability you see is the result of an enormous post-training engineering stack
— RLHF, CoT elicitation, RAG, tool use, system prompts, factuality tuning —
layered on top of a fundamentally noisy base.

The right mental model:

- **Pretraining** sets the signal ceiling — the maximum the model can ever know
  or reason, bounded by what appears in training data
- **Post-training engineering** is noise reduction — extracting cleaner signal
  from a noisy base; it cannot raise the ceiling
- **The base model is trained on the average of human knowledge**, not the best
  of it. Reddit is 40% of training citations. The training signal is frequency
  and plausibility, not truth.

When benchmarks improve quarter over quarter, you are often watching the
engineering get better at extraction, not the ceiling rise. This is why
ARC-AGI-3 — designed to resist post-training tricks — sits at 0.37% AI vs 100%
human. That is closer to the honest ceiling measurement.

The practical implication: **the model's factual accuracy is bounded by what the
average internet user states confidently**, not by what experts have
established. Best current models are wrong on 28-45% of simple factual questions
(SimpleQA Verified). The engineering hides this in normal use. In niche,
specialized, or novel domains — where you most need reliability — it surfaces.

### Medium Term: Broader Economic Risk + AGI Question (~2028—2033)

This is the high-uncertainty zone. Whether broader white-collar disruption
materializes depends on several IF-chains being satisfied:

- **IF** multi-agent systems improve beyond current compounding-error ceiling
- **IF** AI achieves reliable long-horizon task coherence
- **IF** the verification problem is solved in open domains (law, medicine,
  strategy)
- **THEN** roles like financial analyst, paralegal, junior manager, and market
  research analyst face meaningful displacement

Even under optimistic assumptions, Acemoglu (2024) estimates 0.66% TFP gain over
10 years — significant but not transformative. The productivity gains accrue
unevenly: firms that integrate well outcompete those that don't, but aggregate
job losses are slower than the hype suggests.

AGI in this window requires solving problems that current architectures have not
cracked: persistent long-horizon coherence, calibrated uncertainty, genuine
novel reasoning, and open-domain verification. These are not incrementally
closer than they were two years ago.

### Long Term: ASI and Recursive Self-Improvement (~2033+)

The AI 2027 scenario requires a precise chain of events to unfold without a
single defeater: SC by early 2027, SAR by late 2027, and ASI by end of 2027 —
nine months from superhuman coding to superintelligence. Their model has no
uncertainty bands, no structural break analysis, and no branch where SC fails to
arrive.

The honest long-term position: **we do not know.** The chain is possible but not
assured. The prior, based on 50,000—150,000 researchers working for 8 years
without finding a post-transformer paradigm, is that paradigm-shifting discovery
is not a classical search problem and AI has not yet shown the properties needed
to do it.

## Practical Advice: What to Do Now

The common thread across all roles: **move up the stack from execution to
judgment, translation, and verification.** AI is fastest at the bottom of the
stack and slowest at the top. Build irreplaceable institutional knowledge.

### For Data Scientists

**Highest protection:**

- Problem framing and business translation — understanding what question is
  worth asking, what data actually exists, and what a model output means for a
  real decision. AI can draft the analysis; it cannot own the framing.
- Uncertainty communication — explaining to a non-technical executive what the
  model doesn't know, and why that matters. AI cannot calibrate confidence in
  context.
- Verification and quality judgment — the oracle role. For DS outputs there are
  no unit tests; the expert IS the test. If you can reliably tell when the model
  is wrong, you are irreplaceable.
- Brownfield institutional knowledge — undocumented pipelines, political context
  around data, organizational history. AI has none of this.

**Highest risk:**

- Pure execution tasks: EDA, standard feature engineering, model training on
  well-specified problems, standard visualization. Develop AI fluency here but
  do not anchor your value here.

**Skills to build now:**

- AI workflow orchestration: direct AI tools rather than compete with them.
  Learn to specify problems precisely enough that AI execution is reliable.
- Causal and experimental design skills: AI is weak at causal inference,
  experiment design, and interpreting unexpected results — these are defensible.
- Domain depth: the deeper your domain expertise (healthcare, finance,
  manufacturing), the harder you are to replace. Generic DS skills compress;
  domain-grounded DS judgment does not.

**What to prioritize in continuous learning:**

- **Deepen (permanent moat):** Bayesian statistics, causal inference, and causal
  modeling (Pearl's do-calculus / potential outcomes). Correlation mining is
  trivially automated; causal reasoning requires structural assumptions a human
  must defend. Mathematical optimization (Gurobi, LP/MIP) and simulation (MDP,
  MCMC) — problems with verifiable objective functions are both automatable AND
  auditable, making your oversight role high-value.
- **Reframe, don't drop:** Classical ML and deep learning — study _when and why_
  not _how to implement_. The relevant question is "which model class fits this
  problem and how do I evaluate what the AI built?" Same for time series: know
  when classical decomposition beats DL and why.
- **Rising value:** Graph methods (GraphRAG is already production), classical AI
  search and constraint satisfaction (underpins AlphaEvolve-style hybrid
  systems), and game theory (multi-agent AI systems are fundamentally
  game-theoretic). Add uncertainty quantification (conformal prediction,
  calibration) as an explicit track — directly relevant to auditing AI outputs.
- **Lower ROI:** Standard EDA/aggregation implementation, basic library APIs.
  These are where AI excels. Learn the concepts; let the tools do the code.

### For Data Engineers

- **Architecture and system design** remain human-anchored. AI can write
  pipeline code; it cannot specify data contracts, design for unknown future
  requirements, or navigate organizational data ownership.
- **Data quality, governance, and observability** are high-value skills.
  AI-generated pipelines increase the need for humans who can tell when the data
  is wrong.
- **Learn to orchestrate AI-generated code** rather than write all pipelines
  manually. Your value is in knowing what to build, not in writing every line.

### For DevOps / SRE / Platform Engineers

- Infrastructure design, reliability engineering, and incident response require
  human judgment about system behavior under failure — AI assists but cannot own
  on-call decisions.
- **Observability and capacity planning** are high-signal skills as AI workloads
  create new infrastructure patterns (GPU clusters, inference optimization, cost
  management).
- Focus on the orchestration layer: as AI generates more code and more
  pipelines, someone must ensure the systems running them are reliable.

### For Software Engineers

- **The role is shifting from code-writer to code-director.** Senior skills
  (architecture, system design, requirements elicitation, brownfield navigation)
  are far safer than junior skills (greenfield feature coding).
- **Accountability for system behavior** is insulating. AI cannot be responsible
  for production failures; humans must own this.
- Learn to treat AI as a junior engineer: direct precisely, review critically,
  catch the errors it doesn't know it made.
- Specialize in domains where correctness is hard to verify automatically:
  distributed systems, security, performance engineering, embedded systems.

### Generalist vs. Specialist vs. Manager

The dangerous position is **shallow generalist** — broad knowledge with no
depth. AI replaces shallow broad knowledge first; that is exactly what
next-token prediction does well. Pure specialism is stronger: deep expertise in
a niche domain means thin training data, hard-to-verify outputs, and rare edge
cases — all AI-resistant properties.

The strongest position is **π-shaped**: two deep specializations with broad
connective tissue between them. One stem is your technical depth (DS, SWE, DE);
the other is your domain depth (healthcare, finance, manufacturing). With two
stems you can direct AI agents across both domains, synthesize across them in
ways a single-domain specialist cannot, and verify outputs in both. The breadth
lets you orchestrate; the depth lets you catch errors.

**Managing AI-human hybrid teams** is an emerging and undervalued skill. As
technical execution compresses, someone must own: directing agents, verifying
outputs, translating requirements across the human-AI boundary, and making the
judgment calls when the AI produces something nobody fully understands. This
role combines technical fluency, domain expertise, and soft skills — and is
precisely the combination hardest to hire for. The career arc that makes sense:
build technical depth early (you cannot direct what you don't understand),
accumulate domain expertise, then deliberately develop the management and soft
skills that anchor the human interface layer. Trust, organizational navigation,
and accountability are not automatable — and as execution compresses, they
become the whole job for senior people.

### Universal Advice Across All Roles

1. **Become the oracle.** The verification and judgment layer is the most
   durable. Know when AI is wrong.
2. **Build organizational depth.** Relationships, institutional history, and
   political context are outside the training distribution of any model.
3. **Don't compete on speed.** Competing with AI on task execution speed is a
   losing strategy. Compete on judgment, context, and accountability.
4. **Watch the real signals.** SC/RSI requires AI to make genuine discoveries —
   spontaneous anomaly detection, novel verified hypotheses, ARC-AGI-2
   performance approaching human level. Until you see these, the long-term
   scenario remains conditional on assumptions that have not been met.
5. **The plateau is your friend.** If current scaling methods hit their ceiling
   before SC arrives — and there are strong reasons to think they will — the
   disruption stays in the short-term regime. Watch METR for signs of plateau,
   not just continued growth.

## Mythos - April 2026

### Capabilities and Benchmarks

Claude Mythos Preview (April 7, 2026) is Anthropic's most capable model to date
and the first released under RSP v3.0. It is not publicly available — restricted
to ~50 partners (AWS, Apple, Google, Microsoft) for defensive cybersecurity use
only under Project Glasswing. Benchmarks against Opus 4.6:

| Benchmark              | Mythos | Opus 4.6 |
| ---------------------- | ------ | -------- |
| SWE-bench Verified     | 93.9%  | 80.8%    |
| SWE-bench Pro          | 77.8%  | 53.4%    |
| SWE-bench Multilingual | 87.3%  | 77.8%    |
| SWE-bench Multimodal   | 59%    | 27.1%    |
| Terminal-Bench 2.0     | 82%    | 65.4%    |
| GPQA Diamond           | 94.5%  | 91.3%    |

The benchmark profile is telling: coding and agentic tasks improved
dramatically; knowledge and reasoning tasks improved modestly. This is not the
signature of a new pretraining run, which would lift all benchmarks roughly
uniformly. It is the signature of targeted post-training on long-horizon agentic
coding tasks. Anthropic publishes only selected benchmarks. The narrow
publication is itself a signal — a general intelligence breakthrough would show
broad improvement. The omitted benchmarks almost certainly show modest gains.

### What Anthropic Probably Did

The most likely explanation for Mythos, in rough order of confidence:

1. **Extended thinking budget (COT scaling).** The reported price ($25/$125 per
   million tokens) is consistent with extremely long thinking chains. Allowing
   the model to think far longer before responding produces large gains on hard
   agentic tasks and modest gains on retrieval tasks — exactly the observed
   profile. This is inference compute substituting for training compute, and it
   is expensive by design. The performance improvement is likely logarithmic in
   thinking budget: doubling thinking time does not double performance.

2. **RL from verifiable rewards (RLVR) on agentic coding trajectories.** Rather
   than short question-answer pairs, training on long agentic sequences where
   the terminal reward is "did the code pass the tests?" This is a subset of
   RLHF where the human is replaced by an automated oracle. Technically hard
   because credit assignment across hundreds of steps is a difficult problem; a
   breakthrough in doing this efficiently would explain the coding-specific
   jump.

3. **Better synthetic post-training data.** Generating synthetic coding problems
   with verifiable solutions at scale. Note: synthetic data is harmful in
   pretraining (model collapse) but useful in post-training where an oracle can
   verify correctness, preventing error propagation.

### A Note on the Training Taxonomy

The earlier section of this document lists four ways to improve a model:
pretraining, CoT/inference, RLHF, and engineering. Two clarifications worth
adding:

**Fine-tuning and Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** are not separate categories —
they are the _mechanism_ by which RLHF and post-training signals update the
weights. SFT specifically means training on labeled examples of correct behavior
(show the model good trajectories; update weights toward them). It sits between
pretraining and RLHF in the pipeline. Whether SFT exhibits logarithmic returns
like COT is unclear — the honest answer is we don't know, because labs don't
publish ablations on this. The intuition is that SFT returns should also
diminish as the easy corrections are made and you're fighting the messy middle
of the pretrained distribution.

**RLVR (RL from verifiable rewards)** is arguably a fifth distinct signal source
worth naming separately from RLHF. RLHF uses human preference judgments as the
reward signal. RLVR uses ground-truth binary outcomes (pass/fail on a test,
correct/incorrect on a math proof). The oracle replaces the human. This is what
drives coding and math improvements most directly and is qualitatively different
from human preference optimization.

### Pretraining is Signal; Post-Training is Signal Extraction

A useful framing for the whole trajectory of model improvement:

**Pretraining** encodes the average of human knowledge — messy, contradictory,
Reddit-weighted. This is the ceiling on what the model can know. The pretrained
base model would not rank in the top 100 on any benchmark because the signal is
buried in noise.

**Post-training** (SFT, RLHF, RLVR, COT) does not raise the ceiling. It extracts
and amplifies correct signals already latent in the pretrained weights by
reweighting the model's distribution toward verified correct answers. Each
successive post-training technique is a better extraction method applied to the
same underlying base.

The implication: the improvement we have seen across model generations is
substantially improvement in _extraction_, not improvement in the underlying
knowledge base. The ceiling is set at pretraining; the engineering is noise
reduction. This predicts a plateau as extraction techniques exhaust the
recoverable signal — not a sudden wall but a gradual flattening as marginal
gains require exponentially more post-training compute.

### The Cybersecurity Finding — Impressive But Misunderstood

Mythos autonomously found a 27-year-old OpenBSD vulnerability and a 16-year-old
FFmpeg flaw. This sounds like the model reading code and recognizing a subtle
bug. It almost certainly is not.

The actual mechanism: an agentic harness with a verifiable reward signal (did
the exploit succeed?) running for extended periods across a large attack
surface. The model is doing _guided search_ — reasoning about where
vulnerabilities are likely, generating candidate exploits, testing them,
updating — not one-shot insight. This is the same verifiable-reward paradigm
that drives coding improvements, extended to an adversarial domain. The oracle
is "did we get in?"

This is impressive because the search is _guided by reasoning_, not random
fuzzing. Dumb fuzzers have not found these vulnerabilities despite decades of
automated testing. The model's semantic understanding of program execution
directs the search in ways purely random approaches cannot.

It is less impressive than "the model read the code and immediately saw the
bug." That would be a qualitative capability breakthrough. What we have is
better-guided search with more thinking time.

**Crucially**: other frontier labs almost certainly have comparable capability
or are close. The benchmarks include GPT-5.4 and Gemini 3.1 Pro. Anthropic's own
card notes Opus 4.6 was already approaching some of these capabilities. What
made Mythos specifically visible is that Anthropic: (a) set up the agentic
cybersecurity scaffold and ran it (b) gave the model enough thinking budget to
find things (c) chose to publish

OpenAI and Google may not have set up the same adversarial scaffold. If they
had, they may have seen similar results with their own frontier models. This is
a transparency asymmetry, not necessarily a capability asymmetry.

### Why Didn't They Have This Sooner?

The smooth capability trajectory visible in Anthropic's internal AECI plot (Fig
2.3.6.B) raises a question: if improvement is continuous, why release Opus 4.6
and then Mythos separately rather than releasing Mythos directly?

Two mechanisms drive the smooth trend:

**Search over post-training recipe space.** The optimal combination of data
mixture, RL formulation, reward design, and training sequence is not known in
advance. Each model release represents the result of a search over this
combinatorial space. The next search starts from what was learned. This is
inherently sequential — you cannot skip ahead without running the intermediate
experiments that teach you where to search next. Jumping from 2x to 40x would
require a 40x harder search, and the search is the bottleneck.

**Monotonic scaling of post-training resources.** More RLHF comparisons, more
synthetic problems, more RL episodes, more thinking budget. This scales smoothly
with investment and produces smooth returns. Labs invest proportional resources
per generation and get proportional returns — producing the smooth curve for the
same reason Moore's Law was smooth: it reflects economic investment decisions as
much as engineering discovery.

A statistical note on Figure 2.3.6.B: Anthropic claims a changepoint at Mythos
indicating accelerating capability growth. This claim should be treated
skeptically. The changepoint is at the endpoint of the series — exactly where
IRT calibration uncertainty is largest and error bars are widest. A two-piece
linear fit will always find a break somewhere; without a proper likelihood ratio
test penalizing the extra degree of freedom, this is not evidence of structural
acceleration. The incentive to find a changepoint is also not neutral: it
justifies the restricted release and the RSP framework.

### RSP Threshold — Not Crossed, But Less Confident Than Ever

Anthropic concludes Mythos does not cross the automated AI R&D threshold
(compressing 2 years of progress into 1). The evidence:

- Internal use shows it cannot substitute for senior Research Scientists and
  Engineers
- Geometric mean productivity uplift ~4× on individual tasks but this does not
  translate to 2× research progress acceleration — they estimate 40× individual
  task uplift would be needed
- Early claims of AI-attributable research wins "did not hold up" on inspection
  — what looked like autonomous discovery was, on examination, reliable
  execution of human-specified approaches

They hold this conclusion with _less confidence than for any prior model_ —
explicitly flagged. The signal ceiling framing predicts this: better extraction
of coding knowledge does not straightforwardly translate to AI R&D acceleration,
because the bottleneck in research is paradigm-breaking ideas, not code
execution speed. A model that writes code 4× faster still cannot generate the
conceptual frame that produces the next Transformer.

## How Models Actually Improve — And What That Means for the Frontier — April 2026

### The Training Taxonomy

A cleaner taxonomy of how models improve, building on the earlier section:

**Pretraining** encodes the average of human knowledge from internet-scale data.
Messy, contradictory, Reddit-weighted. Sets the knowledge ceiling. Quality
matters as much as quantity — Llama 3's jump over Llama 2 was substantially a
data curation story, not a parameter count story. Better pretraining raises the
ceiling; everything else is extraction from a fixed base. Frontier labs are
almost certainly not doing new pretraining runs between model releases — the
cost is hundreds of millions of dollars and months of compute. The cadence of
releases is too fast for that.

**Post-training signal sources** (operating on the pretrained base):

- **SFT (Supervised Fine-Tuning):** Show the model labeled examples of correct
  behavior — good reasoning chains, correct answers, good agentic trajectories.
  Update weights toward them. Warm-starts the model for RLVR. Returns are
  probably logarithmic as easy corrections are made and the remaining errors are
  in the messy middle of the pretrained distribution.

- **RLHF (RL from Human Feedback):** Human raters judge output quality; a reward
  model is trained on their preferences; RL updates weights against that proxy.
  Expensive, noisy, subject to Goodhart's Law (optimizing the proxy rather than
  the actual preference). Scales poorly because human judgment is the
  bottleneck.

- **RLVR (RL from Verifiable Rewards):** An automated oracle replaces the human
  — did the code pass the tests? Did the proof check out? Did the exploit work?
  Binary, scalable, no reward model approximation. This is what drives coding
  and math improvements. Cannot be applied to domains without an oracle (open-
  ended writing, novel scientific reasoning, aesthetic judgment).

- **Inference-time COT:** No weight update. Token budget is extended at query
  time, buying performance with compute. The model trained with RLVR on hard
  problems learns to self-allocate thinking budget — it discovers that spending
  more steps on harder problems increases reward. This is qualitatively
  different from just setting a long inference budget: the trained model knows
  _when_ to think longer; the naive version burns compute on every query
  regardless. COT scaling is logarithmic — doubling thinking time does not
  double performance.

Fine-tuning is the _mechanism_ (weight update), not a separate signal source.
All of SFT, RLHF, and RLVR are fine-tuning. The distinction is in what signal
drives the update.

### How RLVR Sets Are Made — And Why This Bottlenecks at the Frontier

RLVR requires a verifiable oracle. Public sources exist: competition math
archives, LeetCode/Codeforces, formal proof libraries (Lean, Isabelle), GitHub
issues with test suites. But these are finite and increasingly contaminated once
public — a benchmark that has been out for two years is partially memorized.

**Synthetic generation** extends the set: generate a problem, solve it with a
known correct method, use the solution as ground truth, train on the problem.
Labs use the model itself to calibrate difficulty — discard problems the model
always solves (too easy) and problems it never solves in N attempts (too hard).
This produces a curriculum at the frontier of the model's current capability.

**The frontier bottleneck** is exact: as the model improves, it needs harder
problems for continued training signal. Harder problems require more expert
human knowledge to generate. At the true knowledge frontier there are by
definition fewer published problems, fewer qualified humans to generate them,
and slower experimental feedback loops to verify solutions. This is the RLVR
analogue of METR's task-creation problem at long horizons — it is hard to make
the tests that are very hard. Both converge on the same structural limit: the
frontier is sparse by definition.

### Three Cases of Model Capability — And What They Can Actually Reach

**Case 1: Fact retrieval from pretraining.** The answer is in the weights. No
reasoning required. Already showing clear limits — SimpleQA error rates of
28-45% on simple facts from a messy training distribution. This ceiling is
well-understood.

**Case 2: One-shot bootstrapped reasoning.** The model applies internalized
reasoning strategies to a novel problem in a single forward pass, without
external verification or search. Gets a physics problem right not because it
memorized the answer but because it has internalized enough structure to compose
a correct novel derivation. This is the crucial intermediate case — beyond
retrieval, before agentic search.

**Case 3: Agentic search with oracle.** The model generates candidates, receives
binary feedback from the environment, updates search direction, iterates. Mythos
cybersecurity, code against test suites, AlphaEvolve. The intelligence is in the
_guided search_ — the model's reasoning makes the search smarter than brute
force. The oracle is doing essential work: without it, the model cannot
distinguish correct from incorrect candidates.

**Case 1** is largely saturated and limited by pretraining data quality.

**Case 3** is real and improving, but structurally limited to domains with fast
verifiable oracles. Mythos finding a 27-year-old vulnerability is Case 3 —
agentic search with a binary oracle (did the exploit succeed?), not one-shot
code comprehension. This is impressive because the search is semantically
guided. It is not the model reading code and immediately recognizing a subtle
bug. Other frontier labs almost certainly have comparable capability — what
Anthropic did was build the scaffold and give the model sufficient thinking
budget to run the search. Past the knowledge frontier, Case 3 also stalls: the
oracle for "is this a correct theory of quantum gravity?" is years of
experimental physics, not a unit test. The search loop that works in seconds for
code doesn't work on decade-long experimental timescales.

**Case 2** is the crucial unknown. The model has one genuine potential advantage
over human experts: breadth of recall across domains simultaneously. A human
specialist has read perhaps 10,000 papers deeply; the model has shallow exposure
to millions. The hope is that some frontier problems require connecting two
fields that specialists rarely read across. This is a real but narrow advantage
— the low-hanging cross-domain fruit is probably already picked, and most
remaining frontier problems are fundamental conceptual barriers within
well-explored literatures, not missed analogies between them.

The model has no epistemic advantage in _reasoning strategy_. Its reasoning IS
human reasoning — distilled from everything humans wrote about hs is coherent
but the Mythos card undermines ow to think. It cannot reason in ways humans have
never reasoned. It has no alien perspective, no different cognitive
architecture. When it reasons about physics it is recombining reasoning moves
that physicists made and wrote down. Emergence doesn't escape this: emergence
produces new combinations of existing representations, not representations that
were never present in training.

**What Case 2 bootstrapping to the true frontier would require:**

A one-shot answer to an open problem — "here is how you unify the fundamental
forces" — requires the relevant conceptual frame to be latent somewhere in the
pretraining data, extractable by post-training, and composable into a novel
correct answer. For problems at the true frontier this is implausible: the
conceptual frame that solves the problem is by definition one humans haven't
articulated. It cannot be in the training data. No scale of pretraining or RLVR
produces a frame that nobody has ever written down.

The only genuine escape: Case 3 with reality as the oracle — automated
experimental science where the model generates hypotheses and physical
experiments close the loop. This exists in narrow form (materials discovery,
drug candidates) but is blocked by experimental timescales in most important
domains and requires human-designed experimental infrastructure to run.

### Why Improvement Is Smooth — And What That Predicts

The smooth capability curve across model generations reflects two mechanisms:

**Search over recipe space:** The optimal post-training recipe (data mixture, RL
formulation, reward design, curriculum) is not known in advance. Each model
release is the result of a search over this combinatorial space. The search is
inherently sequential — you cannot skip ahead without the intermediate
experiments that teach you where to look. Jumping from 2x to 40x improvement
would require a 40x harder search.

**Monotonic resource scaling:** More RLHF comparisons, more synthetic problems,
more RL episodes, more thinking budget scale smoothly with investment and
produce proportional returns. This alone would produce a smooth curve.

A third possible contributor: **labs are substantially turning up the COT/
inference compute dial and eating the cost.** All labs can improve benchmark
performance by giving models more thinking budget. They don't do this
immediately at each release because it's expensive at scale. The smooth inter-
lab improvement curve may partly reflect economic pacing — labs wait for
competitive pressure before spending on the next level of inference compute —
rather than pure capability discovery. This would explain the eerie synchrony in
improvement rates across labs with different architectures and training
approaches.

**What the smooth trend predicts:** if improvement is driven by monotonic
resource scaling and recipe search over a fixed pretrained base, the curve will
eventually flatten as the extractable signal is exhausted. Not a sudden wall — a
gradual flattening as marginal post-training gains require exponentially more
compute. The logarithmic form means each successive doubling of post-training
investment buys less benchmark improvement than the last. The RLVR frontier
bottleneck (harder to make hard tests) compounds this: as the model improves,
the training signal becomes harder and more expensive to generate.

This is the honest forecasting picture. The smooth trend is real. The question
is whether it reflects a continuing extractable signal or an approaching ceiling
— and current evidence does not cleanly separate these.

### What Economists Actually Forecast — April 2026

Karger, Kuusela, Abaluck, Bryan, Halperin et al. (March 2026), *"Forecasting
the Economic Effects of AI"*, Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago / Forecasting
Research Institute. Structured expert elicitation across five groups: academic
economists, AI industry professionals, AI policy researchers, superforecasters
(top 2% accuracy on geopolitical forecasting), and the general public.

Key findings:

1. **Economists assign only 14% probability to the rapid scenario** (AI surpasses
   humans on most cognitive and physical tasks by 2030). Median is 10%. The modal
   expectation is moderate progress. Dario Amodei's claim of 10-20% unemployment
   within five years is an extreme outlier relative to this distribution.

2. **Unconditional forecasts stay close to historical trends.** Despite expecting
   significant AI progress, median GDP forecast is ~2.5% annually — above
   government baselines but not transformatively so. Economists weight technology
   diffusion lags heavily: general-purpose technologies historically take 20-30
   years to show up in productivity data.

3. **Conditional on rapid scenario, effects are large but not unprecedented.**
   GDP growth rises to 3.5% (economists) and 4.0% (superforecasters) by 2050.
   Labor force participation falls from 62% to 55% — roughly 10 million jobs
   attributable to AI. Wealth held by the top 10% rises to 80%, a historical
   high. Large shifts, but with historical parallels.

4. **AI industry professionals are the most optimistic** — rapid scenario GDP
   median of 5.3% vs. economists' 3.5%. They are also more pessimistic on job
   losses. The revealed preference of the people closest to the technology is
   notably different from independent academic economists.

5. **Superforecasters — the best-calibrated group — are the most conservative.**
   Their rapid scenario GDP forecast (4.0%) is well below AI industry (5.3%).
   This is the Tetlock group whose predictions are actually tracked for accuracy.

6. **The main source of disagreement is not AI timelines — it is the economic
   transmission mechanism.** A variance decomposition shows that expert
   disagreement is driven primarily by different beliefs about what happens
   *given* highly capable AI, not by disagreement about when or whether capable
   AI arrives. Even if everyone agreed on timelines, they would still disagree
   enormously on outcomes because economics-to-capability transmission is deeply
   uncertain.

7. **Economists strongly prefer targeted interventions.** 71.8% support worker
   retraining; only 13.7% support a job guarantee; 37.4% support UBI. The
   general public is much more supportive of broad structural interventions.
   Baumol Cost Disease is explicitly cited as a mechanism constraining aggregate
   gains — sectors without AI penetration remain expensive bottlenecks on overall
   productivity.
